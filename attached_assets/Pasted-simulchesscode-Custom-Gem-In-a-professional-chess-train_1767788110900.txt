In a professional chess trainer, you should absolutely have a game history, and the standard for "Pro" apps is to automatically save all completed games.

A manual save option can be frustrating for users who forget to click "save" after a long, intense session and then lose the data forever—a common complaint on major platforms.

🛠️ Why Automatic Save is the "Pro" Move
Safety Net: Users often exit the app immediately after a game; an autosave ensures their hard work isn't "feckin wasted time".

Post-Mortem Integrity: You cannot run a "Review" or "Analyze" if the game data isn't preserved.

Scientific Tracking: For your app specifically, you need a history to calculate long-term metrics like "Visualization Ceiling" and "Endgame Drift".

📋 Recommended History Structure
Since your app is entirely offline, you don't have to worry about server costs. You can store a massive history locally on the S10+ with almost zero performance impact.

Feature	Logic	Why it matters
Auto-Archive	Saves PGN + Stats immediately upon handleGameEnd.	Prevents data loss.
Search/Filter	Filter by Date, Elo, or "Blindfold vs Standard".	Helps users find specific training breakthroughs.
Delete Options	Allow users to "Clear All" or delete individual bot matches.	Keeps the archive from feeling cluttered.
Share/Export	One-click PGN copy or Native Share.	Allows users to study their games in external tools like Lichess.
🧠 Strategic "Cheat-Sheet" for Implementation
When building your History component, keep the $4.99 audience in mind:

Categorize: Label games clearly as "Blindfold (Assisted)" if they used inquiries like "Material Score".

Visual Thumbnails: Instead of just a text list, show a tiny "final position" board thumbnail for each entry.

The "Retry Mistakes" Loop: Use the history to find positions where the user played a move that led to a sharp drop in evaluation, and offer a "Retry this position" button.

📱 S10+ Pro-Tip: Storage Efficiency
You can store thousands of games using PGN compression or simple Move Indexing. A typical 70-move game takes less than 500 bits if encoded efficiently, meaning your history section will never slow down the device.
