1. History and OriginOrigin: Chess originated in India around the 6th century AD. The earliest known form was called Chaturanga, which means "four divisions" (infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots, represented by the modern pawn, knight, bishop, and rook).The Name: The word "Chess" comes from the Persian word "shāh," meaning "king."Checkmate: The word "checkmate" comes from the Persian phrase "shāh māt," meaning "the king is helpless" or "the king is dead."Introduction to Europe: The game spread through Persia and the Islamic world, arriving in Europe (specifically Spain and Italy) around the 10th century.Queen's Power: The pieces gained their modern movement rules (especially the powerful Queen and Bishop) in the late 15th century, during the Renaissance. This version is sometimes called "Queen's Chess" or "Mad Queen Chess."First World Champion: The first official World Chess Champion was Wilhelm Steinitz, who claimed the title in 1886.2. Mathematics and PossibilityShannon Number: The Shannon Number is an estimate of the complexity of chess, calculated as $10^{120}$. This represents the estimated number of possible unique games.Game Length: The longest tournament game ever played was between Ivan Nikolić and Goran Arsović in 1989, lasting 20 hours and 15 minutes over 269 moves (ending in a draw).No Draws Rule (Record): A rule existed that prohibited draws before move 50. The longest game played under this rule lasted 193 moves between Karpov and Kaidanov (1993).Starting Moves: From the initial position, White has 20 possible first moves. After the first pair of moves, there are 400 possible positions.Average Game: The average chess game lasts about 40 moves.Longest Forced Mate: The longest known forced mate in a composed problem is over 500 moves, though in practical play, the 50-move rule usually forces a draw long before that.3. Pieces and RulesFifty-Move Rule: If 50 moves pass without either a pawn move or a capture, a player may claim a draw. This prevents extremely long, tedious endgames where no progress is being made.Promotion: A pawn is the only piece that changes identity. When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it must be promoted to a Queen, Rook, Bishop, or Knight (typically a Queen).En Passant: This is a special pawn capture rule invented in the 15th century. It allows a pawn to capture an opponent's pawn that has moved two squares forward on its first move, treating it as if it had only moved one square.Castling: This is the only move in chess where two pieces (King and Rook) move simultaneously, and the only time a King moves two squares.4. Modern and Cultural FactsFIDE: The global governing body for chess is the Fédération Internationale des Échecs (FIDE), founded in Paris on July 20, 1924. This date is now celebrated as International Chess Day.Grandmaster Title: The Grandmaster (GM) title is the highest title a chess player can achieve, awarded by FIDE.Youngest GM: The youngest player ever to earn the Grandmaster title is Abhimanyu Mishra (USA), who achieved the title at the age of 12 years, 4 months, and 25 days in 2021.Highest Rating: The highest classical Elo rating ever achieved is 2882, held by Magnus Carlsen (Norway).Longest Reign: Emanuel Lasker (2nd World Champion) held the title for the longest period: 27 years (1894–1921).Gender: Judit Polgár (Hungary) is widely considered the strongest female player of all time and is the only woman to have broken the 2700 Elo barrier and played for the World Championship title.Computer Chess: In 1997, the IBM computer Deep Blue became the first machine to defeat a reigning World Champion (Garry Kasparov) in a classical match format.5. Chess in SpaceFirst Game in Space: The first game of chess played in space occurred in 1970 between the crew of the Soviet spacecraft Soyuz 9 (Andrian Nikolayev and Vitaly Sevastyanov) and a team on Earth (Viktor Gorbatko and Nikolay Kamanin). It ended in a draw.

Pieces, Notation, and EquipmentThe Algebric Notation (e.g., e4, Nf3) was standardized in the 19th century; before that, Descriptive Notation was common.In Descriptive Notation, the square c1 would be known as QR1 (Queen's Rook 1). A Bishop Pair is often considered a long-term advantage in the endgame, especially in open positions.The King piece has the special notation 'K' in English, while the Knight uses 'N' to avoid confusion with the King.The Knight is often nicknamed "The Horse" in common chess parlance.The term "Battery" refers to lining up two pieces (often Queen and Bishop/Rook) to attack a target along the same rank, file, or diagonal.The earliest forms of the chess clock were sandglasses, used at the London Tournament in 1862.Modern tournaments use digital clocks capable of enforcing increment or delay time controls (e.g., Fischer Delay).Fischer Delay time control, invented by Bobby Fischer, gives players a fixed time delay before their clock starts counting down per move.Increment time control, popularized by FIDE, adds a fixed amount of time (e.g., 30 seconds) to a player's clock after every move.Chess pieces are traditionally made of Boxwood for the light pieces and Ebony for the dark pieces.The most common size for tournament chess squares is 2 to 2.25 inches (5.1–5.7 cm).III. Theory, Tactics, and EndgamesThe "Smothered Mate" is a checkmate delivered by a Knight when the opposing King is completely surrounded (smothered) by its own pieces.The "Epaulette Mate" is a checkmate where the King is restricted on both sides by its own Rooks (like epaulets on a uniform).A "Fork" is a tactical maneuver where a single piece (usually a Knight) simultaneously attacks two or more opponent pieces.A "Pin" is a tactic where an attacking piece makes it inadvisable or illegal for a defending piece to move because a valuable piece (often the King) is behind it.The "Skewer" is the opposite of a Pin; a valuable piece is attacked, and if it moves, a less valuable piece behind it will be captured.Opposition is a fundamental endgame technique where the Kings are separated by an odd number of squares on a rank or file, used to gain access to key squares.The Triangulation technique is used in the King-and-Pawn endgame to lose a tempo and place the opponent in zugzwang.The Lucena Position is a key theoretical winning position in Rook and Pawn vs. Rook endgames.The Philidor Position is a key theoretical drawing position in Rook and Pawn vs. Rook endgames.The term "Doctrinaire" is often used to describe dogmatic players who strictly adhere to rigid opening principles."Hanging Pawns" are two adjacent pawns (e.g., on c4 and d4) with no friendly pawns on the adjacent files (b and e) that can defend them.The "Backward Pawn" is a pawn that cannot be defended by any other pawn and can be easily blockaded or attacked.The term "Outpost" refers to a square protected by a pawn on the opponent's side of the board, often occupied by a Knight.Pawn Structure refers to the arrangement of pawns, which is the skeleton of the position and often determines the middlegame plan.IV.
